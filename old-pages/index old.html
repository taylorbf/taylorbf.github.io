about

Ben Taylor is an interdisciplinary artist and creative coder who specializes in web art, web audio, and networked performance practices. His research investigates the way ideas translate between the arts, and how we can apply that history to guide the artistic use of networks and new media. He likes to build toolkits that help other people make music in web browsers (nexusosc.com), and occasionally uses those toolkits himself, with varying success.

Ben has presented his research internationally at conferences and festivals including the Pixilerations New Media Festival (Brown/RISD, 2011), New Interfaces for Musical Expression (Seoul 2013, London 2014), Web Audio Conference (IRCAM/Mozilla 2015), Leaders in Software and Art (NY 2011), Music for People and Thingamajigs (2010), International Conference on Live Coding (McMaster Univ, 2016), International Computer Music Conference (2015), and others. He is a founding member of the Louisiana Mobile App Orchestra (LMAO), and his music has been released by the Society for Electroacoustic Music in the United States (SEAMUS).

He received an M.F.A. In Electronic Music & Recording Media from Mills College and has studied with members of the League of Automatic Music Composers and The Hub, Brian Harnetty, and Pauline Oliveros. In 2016, he earned a Ph.D. in Experimental Music & Digital Media from LSU, while doing research at the LSU Center for Computation and Technology.

When not making art with the web, Ben is a developer and CTO at bitdreams.us, and teaches creative coding at Goucher College Digital Arts.

October, 2016
I recently returned from ICLC -- the Live Coding Conference, hosted at McMaster University in Ontario -- and I'm inspired to share an update. ICLC was full of ideas, passionate work, and a surprisingly diverse array of performances. Visual art, choreography, web browsers, and custom languages abounded. I performed The Last CLoud, a live net art performance in which I live code multiple browser windows.

I've been up to a lot over the past year. I defended my dissertation, chaired the music & art tracks of the 2016 web audio conference, had a son, and have been actively designing and presenting new creative code projects.

This spring I collaborated with a former student, Andrew Bernstein, to develop two web audio toolkits, which we shared together at the Web Audio Conference --

  • Tune.js: A collection of 3,500 microtonal and historical tunings, ported to JavaScript. It includes a small API for using these microtonal scales in web audio apps
  • Gendy.js: A web audio implementation of Iannis Xenakis' gendy-N algorithm. It is an oscillator whose waveform nodes meander in a "drunk" walk. Quite fun!
Currently teaching a Python course at Goucher, covering new media topics like image processing, twitter bots, and data visualization. I'm also writing a browser-based DAW for a new media firm, Create1, in New York.

Upcoming projects for the winter:

  • Researching the history of using an audience's mobile devices as a speaker array, including Jose Maceda's 1974 Ugnayan, Golan Levin et al's 2000s Dialtones series, and many more contemporary works. It is really a fascinating history taking shape before our eyes
  • A live coding environment for generating algorithmic piano patterns and gestures, using data-to-music API by Taka, and a stellar web audio piano Yotam Mann at Tone.js.
  • A fork of NexusUI, with fewer, more powerful interfaces, as well as tuning and timing APIs. This is branching out of a library I use for my own compositional practice.
 
 
April 15, 2015
Recent happenings:

In December I released NexusUI 1.0, a major update, just in time for the 1st Web Audio Conference. I travelled to Paris to present at the conference with Jesse Allison. We presented an in-progress project BRAID, which is a way to build audio instruments by drag-and-drop right in the web browser. The conference was at the legendary electronic music studio IRCAM, which was incredible to see. The conference included talks about some cool new projects including (but not limited to) Lissajous, Meyda, and Tone.JS.

I also participated in a concert, at the Mozilla Paris office, of distributed web audio performance, meaning all sounds were created through the mobile devices of the audience. I performed a version of my networked piece, Pearl River. Of particular note, though, was a piece Fields, by Tim Shaw and Sebastien Piquemal, which has been touring for a few years now. The piece, and the concert, felt like a new way of listening. I expect this is a performance paradigm we'll be seeing more of in the years to come.

Now, my work is transitioning to a new project, tentatively titled McLuhan.js, which will be a JavaScript library for making browser art. The hope is to offer simplified methods for doing the kind of web art that I'm so interested in.

Oh, and another new project (what am I doing to myself...) tentatively titled ToneJam, which I hope to turn into a tool for improvisatory distributed computer music performance.

Other misc notes:

• I recently completed an independent study of sorts, on the relationship between visual art and music in the 20th century. If you're interested in this subject, you may enjoy my blog on the subject, linked to there. First posts are at the bottom.

• Teaching web development at Goucher this spring. More specifically, teaching how to think in code.

• Getting ready for the 2015 New Interfaces for Musical Expression conference, which we're hosting at LSU!

November 3, 2013

I'm happy to report that a short piece of computer music that I composed this summer, inflected dreams has been accepted for the upcoming SEAMUS electroacoustic "miniatures" CD. SEAMUS is the Society for Electroacoustic Music in the United States, and I'm really pleased to be able to be involved with the project. Especially excited to see what other composers came up with for this year's theme, negative space. Thank you to the panel of judges and please check out the CD when it is released this winter!

In other news, the Laptop Orchestra of Louisiana has an upcoming concert on November 11 at the LSU Shaver Theater. We'll be playing all kinds of odd sounds and musics, many that send data through the network as a means to create compositional form. My piece DaisyHead - made entirely with Web Audio API and node.js - will be on the concert. More details on the concert here.

Lastly, a story:
In 2011, when I visited New York to present at the wonderful LISA salon, I stayed on the couch of my friend, artist Erik Sanner, in Harlem. The next morning, he woke me up before sunrise, we went to his basement studio, and he asked me to contribute to a painting while having a conversation about art while we videotaped ourselves painting and having this conversation. Then we ate Cream of Wheat and I drove back to Boston. It was a pretty awesome experience, mostly because of how much I got to learn from Erik and his artistic process and open-mindedness. Erik brought in several other collaborators into that artwork. Now he is showing the piece at Allegheny College, with the video of those conversations being projected back onto the painting. Nice!

july 16, 2013

Excited to be able to share Nexus UI, a JavaScript library of user-interface components for making music with websites and mobile phones, developed with Jesse Allison and Yemin Oh.

www.nexusosc.com

Nexus UI makes it easy to create a musical interface in a website, accessible by web browser or mobile phone. You can either connect it to the new Web Audio API, or have the interface send OSC messages to your Max patch or any musical software with an OSC listener. Nexus is full of fun toggles, dials, multisliders, accelerometer sensors, and bouncy balls. Check it out! Feedback is more than welcome!!
